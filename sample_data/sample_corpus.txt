The Clockwork Orchard

On the edge of Larkspur City there was an orchard that never grew fruit.
Instead, every spring it bloomed with tiny brass gears and copper leaves.
The trees clicked gently in the wind, like polite applause for the sun.

Mara had heard the stories: if you listened closely enough, you could learn
something true about the future. Not predictions, exactly—more like hints.
A creak in a branch might mean “be patient.” A burst of bright chiming might
mean “say yes.”

She brought a notebook and a thermos of tea, and she sat between two
old apple-gear trees. The air smelled of warm metal and rain.

Click. Click-click. Click.

Mara wrote down the rhythm, then paused, then wrote it again with different
spacing. She had been an engineer before the flood barriers failed, before
the city learned to live with water in the streets. Now she repaired small
things: radios, door latches, the occasional bicycle.

In the orchard, the rhythm shifted.

Click… click… click-click-click.

“Three, two, three,” she whispered. A pattern. A sequence. The orchard was
teaching her in the only language it knew: repetition with variation.

She looked up and saw a new branch forming at the tip of one tree, unfolding
with a slow, deliberate twist. On its end, something hung like a lantern.
It wasn’t a fruit. It was a little sealed capsule, no bigger than her palm,
etched with numbers that looked like coordinates.

Mara stood. Her boots sank slightly into soft soil. She reached for the capsule,
and it warmed as her fingers touched it.

Inside, wrapped in oiled paper, was a thin strip of metal printed with a message:

“BUILD THE THING THAT LISTENS. THEN SHARE IT.”

She laughed, startled by how direct it was. The orchard had never been direct.

When she returned to her workshop, she cleared the table. She dug out spare
microphones and old circuit boards. She sketched a device that could record
the orchard’s clicks at high resolution, then translate timing into tokens,
then tokens into meaning—if meaning was there.

Weeks passed. The device slowly took shape. The city’s power flickered, so she
added a hand-crank. The humidity corroded her first design, so she sealed it in resin.
She tested it on dripping pipes and on her own heartbeat.

Finally, on a bright morning, she carried the finished device back to the orchard.

She turned the crank.

The device listened.

And for the first time, Mara felt the orchard listening back.
